Is Race "Real"?

DNA Experiment

By: Bao Nguyen

Part One: Is Race Biologically Real?

Race is not biologically real. While humans have genetic differences, those differences do not form clear biological groups that match the racial categories people use today in society.

In class, we did an experiment to see this for ourselves. We swabbed our cheeks to get some cells and then used a few cool science tools to analyze our DNA. Here is how we did it:

  1. The Swab: We started by taking a sterile swab and rubbing the inside of our cheeks to collect cells. It was pretty simple, but it was the most important part—that’s where our cheek cells were hiding!
  2. Cell Lysis: We mixed those cheek cells with a buffer solution and heated the mixture. This acts like a chemical "key," breaking open the cell membranes and nuclear envelopes to release the DNA from inside the nucleus.
  3. Loading the Gel: We used micropipettes to carefully load our DNA samples into the tiny wells of an agarose gel. This was definitely the hardest part—you have to have a super steady hand to make sure the sample goes into the well and doesn't just float away.
  4. The Run: Once the gel was loaded, we turned on the power supply. DNA has a negative electrical charge because of the phosphate groups in its backbone. Because opposites attract, the DNA migrated through the gel toward the positive (red) electrode.
  5. Analyzing the Results: The gel acts like a dense sponge with tiny tunnels. Smaller DNA strands moved faster through these tunnels, while larger strands stayed closer to the start. By comparing our bands to a "DNA ladder"—a reference standard with known fragment sizes—we could count our repeats.

The results showed that students from different races often had the same number of DNA repeats, while students from the same race often had no similarities in repeats. It was basically a 50/50 split, with no clear pattern connecting race to the genetic variation we tested. We also looked at maps of skin color, blood type, and the sickle cell trait around the world. These showed that traits are distributed independently; for example, people with similar skin colors can have totally different blood types, and the sickle cell trait is actually more related to where malaria was common in history than to the social concept of race. If biological race were real, these traits would be expected to cluster together in consistent, distinct groups, but instead, they overlap and vary gradually across different regions of the world.

The idea of biological race is that humans can be divided into distinct biological groups and that people within the same race are more genetically similar to each other than they are to people of other races. This idea also assumes that visible traits such as skin color, hair texture, or eye shape can reliably identify a person’s race. Genetic variation refers to differences in DNA between individuals. If biological races were real, people within the same race would be expected to share many of the same genetic traits, while people from different races would be expected to have more genetic differences.

The evidence does not support the idea of biological race. In our DNA experiment, people from different racial groups often had the same genetic pattern, while people from the same racial group often had different patterns, being split close to 50/50. This shows that genetic variation does not follow racial categories. The maps of skin color, blood type, and sickle cell trait also show that different traits are distributed independently across populations. If biological race was real, these traits would be expected to form clear groups that match racial categories. Instead, the traits overlap and vary gradually across different regions of the world.

Part Two: Is Race Socially & Psychologically Real?

Race is socially and psychologically real. Even though race is not a clear biological division, people still treat it as meaningful data, treating people of one race as if they are the same, which shapes thoughts, behavior, and life experiences.

The IAT (Implicit Association Test) measures how quickly a person matches ideas and images together. In the test, a person sorts words or pictures into categories as fast as possible. It measures automatic mental connections and biases that are often unknown to the test taker. Typical results for Americans show a stronger automatic connection between White people with positive ideas and Black people with negative ideas, which suggests bias.

Another piece of evidence is the eye-color activity and the other videos we watched. When kids and adults were divided, they treated each other differently based on something random like eye color. They started acting completely differently from how they were just the day before, all because of a simple physical trait. It shows how a group can become "real" in people’s minds even when the trait itself does not create a true biological division.

Race being socially real means people agree to treat it as important and organize their behavior around it. Something is psychologically real when it affects how people think, feel, and react. Race fits both of these ideas. It is shaped by explicit bias, which is open and conscious prejudice, and implicit bias, which is unconscious or automatic. It is also supported by cultural racism, which are the everyday ideas and messages people absorb, and structural racism, which refers to systems and institutions that prefer one ethnic group over another, leading to disadvantages.

The evidence shows that race is socially and psychologically real because people respond to it, even when it is not a biological fact. The IAT shows that many people have automatic associations connected to race, and the eye-color activity shows that people can begin to act differently when a group label is created and reinforced. That means race affects behavior, identity, and social treatment. If race were not socially and psychologically real, then most people would not show automatic racial associations on the IAT, and group labels would not change the way people treat each other or see themselves. Since the evidence shows that people do react to racial categories, race is real in the social and psychological sense.

Extension: How Did Neighborhoods Become Segregated?

Neighborhoods became segregated largely because government and market systems steered people into different areas and kept those patterns going. They often stay that way because the original separation affected housing, wealth, and who could buy where.

On the "Mapping Inequality" website, the government graded neighborhoods by "credit-worthiness" and risk. The green "Grade A" areas were wealthy neighborhoods with large single-family houses and were mostly populated by white families, while the yellow and red "Grade D" areas were smaller-lot neighborhoods populated mostly by people of color and immigrant families. The map was not just describing neighborhoods; it was ranking them in ways tied to race and class, and the rankings were used to guide mortgage lending and real estate development.

In the "Parable of the Polygons," each polygon only moves when it is unhappy, and each one has a small preference for living near similar neighbors. Even with that mild bias, the simulation quickly becomes strongly segregated. The model shows that segregation can emerge from many small individual choices.

Redlining is when lenders, insurers, or government systems treat neighborhoods as risky because of the people who live there, often based on race or class. That makes it harder for families in those neighborhoods to get mortgages, maintain property values, and build wealth. Over time, this can push groups into different neighborhoods and make segregation harder to undo.

The evidence fits a pattern of segregation being created and then reinforced. If segregation were mostly accidental or temporary, the inequality map would not consistently rank the "best" neighborhoods as those with white residents and the "worst" with Black residents and immigrants. Also, the polygon model would not turn mildly mixed neighborhoods into highly segregated ones just from a small preference, and the homeownership gap would not remain so persistent across decades. Instead, the sources show a system that sorts people and keeps segregation going.
